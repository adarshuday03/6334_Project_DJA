\section{Executive Summary}
%==============================================================================

This report analyzes optimal order quantities for SparkFire LLC's ``Seventh Heaven'' sparkler product under demand uncertainty. We examine baseline newsvendor decisions, pricing strategies, promotional incentives, quantity discounts, and two-stage ordering with demand signals. Key ambiguity resolutions are specified task-wise in the Technical Appendix.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Task 1: Conceptual Analysis of Order Quantity}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Based on cost parameters ($p=\$5$, $c=\$3$, $f=0.5$, $s=\$0.50$), we predict $Q^*$ will \textbf{EQUAL} expected demand of 270 units.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Underage Cost:} $C_u = p - c = \$2.00$ per stockout
    \item \textbf{Overage Cost:} $C_o = c(1-f) + s = \$2.00$ per unsold unit
\end{itemize}

Since $C_u = C_o$, costs are perfectly balanced. This symmetric structure places optimal inventory at the distribution median, which equals the mean (270 units) for uniform demand.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Task 2: Optimal Order Quantity Calculation}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Newsvendor Model}

Cost parameters:
\begin{align*}
C_u &= p - c = 5 - 3 = \$2.00 \quad \text{(underage)} \\
C_o &= c(1-f) + s = 3(0.5) + 0.5 = \$2.00 \quad \text{(overage)}
\end{align*}

Critical ratio: $\text{CR} = C_u/(C_u + C_o) = 2.00/4.00 = 0.50$

Optimal quantity for $D \sim \text{Uniform}[120, 420]$:
\[
Q^* = a + (b-a) \times \text{CR} = 120 + 300 \times 0.50 = \boxed{270 \text{ cases}}
\]

Expected profit: $\E[\Pi(Q^*)] = \$370$.

\subsubsection{Reflection: Distribution Assumption Limitation}

The uniform distribution assumes equal likelihood for all demand values, which rarely holds in practice. Alternative distributions impact results:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Normal:} Similar $Q^*$, higher profit (reduced variance)
    \item \textbf{Lognormal:} Higher $Q^*$ (right-skew increases stockout risk)
    \item \textbf{Impact:} Distribution choice significantly affects both optimal policy and profitability
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Task 4: Pricing Decision}

\subsubsection{Optimal Quantity at p = \$6}

With higher price $p = \$6$ (and $f=0.5$):
\begin{itemize}
    \item $C_u = p - c = 6 - 3 = \$3.00$ (increased from \$2.00)
    \item $C_o = c(1-f) + s = \$2.00$ (unchanged)
    \item Critical ratio: $3/(3+2) = 0.60$
    \item $Q^*_{p=6} = 120 + 300 \times 0.60 = \boxed{300 \text{ cases}}$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Task 6: Corvette Prize Incentive}

\subsubsection{(a) Modified Profit Model}

Expected profit with prize:
\[
\E[\Pi_{\text{prize}}(Q)] = \E[\Pi_{\text{base}}(Q)] + \text{Prize} \times P(\text{win} \mid Q)
\]

Using 5\% win probability at sales $\geq 400$ units:
\[
P(\text{win} \mid Q \geq 400) = 0.05 \times P(D \geq 400) = 0.05 \times \frac{20}{300} = 0.00333
\]

Expected prize value at $Q=400$: $\$40{,}000 \times 0.00333 = \$133$.

\subsubsection{(b) Optimal Quantity Q**}
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
$Q$ & Base Profit & Prize EV & Total $\E[\Pi]$ \\
\midrule
270 & \$370 & \$0 & \$370 \\
380 & \$289 & \$160 & \$449 \\
400 & \$257 & \$214 & \$471 \\
420 & \$220 & \$213 & \$433 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Optimal decision:} $Q^{**} = 400$ units maximizes total expected profit at \$471.

\subsubsection{(c) Risk-Seeking Behavior Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $Q^* = 270$ (no prize): Safe, balanced inventory
    \item $Q^{**} = 400$ (with prize): +48\% inventory (+130 units)
    \item Base profit drops \$370 $\to$ \$257 (aggressive overstocking)
    \item Prize EV compensates: Total profit +27\% (\$370 $\to$ \$471)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Incentive Effect:} The prize encourages \textbf{risk-seeking} behavior. SparkFire stocks 130 additional units (97\% increase in leftover inventory from 38 to 131 units) to maximize prize eligibility, accepting 31\% lower base profit for potential windfall.

\subsubsection{Assumption \& Reflection}

\textbf{Prize Rule:} We use 5\% @ 400 units as the primary threshold, consistent with the problem. The 3\% @ 380 and 7\% @ 420 are treated as contextual references from other states.
\textbf{Behavioral Limitation:} Adding expected value (\$133 to \$267) understates psychological impact. The vivid prospect of winning a \$40,000 Corvette likely drives decision-makers to order \textit{beyond} rational $Q^{**}$, as small probabilities are overweighted (Kahneman-Tversky theory). Real ordering may exceed 420 units due to regret aversion and lottery appeal.

\subsection{Task 8: Two-Stage Ordering with Demand Signal}

\subsubsection{(a) Profit Function}

\[
\pi(Q_0, Q_1(S), D) = p \cdot \min(Q_0 + Q_1(S), D) - c_0 \cdot Q_0 - c_1 \cdot Q_1(S) + f \cdot c_0 \cdot \max(Q_0 + Q_1(S) - D, 0) - s \cdot \max(Q_0 + Q_1(S) - D, 0)
\]

where $Q_0$ is pre-season order at $c_0 = \$3.00$, $Q_1(S)$ is expedited order at $c_1 = \$3.60$ after observing signal $S \in \{\text{High}, \text{Low}\}$, and $D$ is realized demand. All refunds at $c_0$ regardless of ordering stage.

\subsubsection{(b) Signal-Specific Optimal Inventory}

Using discrete demand approximation $D \in \{330, 190\}$ and inverse critical ratio $\text{CR} = C_o/(C_o + C_u) = 0.622$ for expedited cost $c_1 = \$3.60$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Signal High:} $Q_H^* = 330$ units, $\E[\pi \mid S = \text{High}] = \$508$
    \item \textbf{Signal Low:} $Q_L^* = 190$ units, $\E[\pi \mid S = \text{Low}] = \$340$
\end{itemize}

Reactive policy: $Q_1^*(S) = \max(0, Q_S^* - Q_0)$

\subsubsection{(c) Stage 1 Optimization}

Backward induction yields optimal pre-season order:

\textbf{Optimal Policy:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $Q_0^* = 190$ units (pre-season at \$3.00)
    \item $Q_1^*(\text{High}) = 140$ units (expedited top-up when signal is High)
    \item $Q_1^*(\text{Low}) = 0$ units (no top-up when signal is Low)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Total Expected Profit:}
\[
\E[\pi] = 0.45 \times 508 + 0.55 \times 340 = \$415.60
\]

\subsubsection{(d) Value of Signal and Reactive Capacity (VOSRC)}

\[
\text{VOSRC} = \$415.60 - \$370.00 = \$45.60 \text{ (12.3\% improvement)}
\]

where \$370.00 is baseline profit from Part 2 (single-stage ordering).

\textbf{Justification:} The VOSRC of \$45.60 demonstrates that paying the \$0.60 expedited premium is rational when the signal accurately predicts high demand (80\% accuracy) and occurs with sufficient frequency (45\% probability). The signal's value derives from enabling profitable reactive top-up orders that capture high-demand opportunities while the conservative pre-season order (190 units) limits downside risk. The strategy is robust because the Low-signal response requires zero additional orders regardless of Low-signal accuracy, making VOSRC insensitive to uncertainty in low-demand scenarios. Expedited ordering becomes economically justified when signal quality exceeds approximately 70\% accuracy for the High signal, which our baseline 80\% comfortably satisfies.

\subsection{Managerial Insights}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The baseline order of 270 cases uses a critical ratio of 0.5, balancing underage and overage risks equally.
    
    \item Price increases have substantial leverage: a \$1 price increase raises optimal profit by 65\%.
    
    \item The Corvette promotion shifts optimal behavior from 207 to 400 cases---a 93\% increase driven by lottery-style incentives.
    
    \item Quantity discounts should be evaluated holistically; the middle tier (\$2.85) outperforms the deepest discount.
    
    \item Two-stage ordering with signal information adds \$45.60 value (12\% improvement) when signal quality is high.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{OE1: Contract Design}

\subsubsection{Current Contract Analysis}

The existing buyback contract with $f = 0.5$ and $s = \$0.50$ creates misaligned incentives:

\textbf{SparkFire's perspective:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Overage cost: $C_o = c(1-f) + s = 3.00(0.5) + 0.50 = \$2.00$
    \item Underage cost: $C_u = p - c = 5.00 - 3.00 = \$2.00$
    \item Generous refund policy encourages overstocking (risk shifted to Leisure Limited)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Leisure Limited's perspective:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Bears cost of refurbishing/reselling returned inventory
    \item High refund rate (\$1.50 per unit) creates financial burden when many stands overorder
    \item No mechanism to share upside when demand is high
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Proposed Two-Parameter Contract: Reduced Buyback + Enhanced Incentives}

\textbf{Parameter 1: Reduce refund fraction}
\begin{itemize}
    \item New refund: $f = 0.35$ (down from 0.50)
    \item New overage cost: $C_o = 3.00(0.65) + 0.50 = \$2.45$
    \item Effect: SparkFire becomes more conservative, reducing Leisure's return burden
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Parameter 2: Enhance quantity discounts or prize}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Option A: Lower discount threshold from 200 to 150 units, or increase discount depth
    \item Option B: Increase Corvette prize from \$40,000 to \$60,000 with clearer qualification rules
    \item Effect: Compensates SparkFire for reduced buyback protection while encouraging volume
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Trade-off Impact}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Contract} & \textbf{$C_o$} & \textbf{$C_u$} & \textbf{Critical Ratio} \\
\midrule
Current ($f = 0.50$) & \$2.00 & \$2.00 & 0.500 \\
Proposed ($f = 0.35$) & \$2.45 & \$2.00 & 0.449 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Overage/underage cost comparison}
\end{table}

\textbf{Alignment benefits:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reduced overordering:} Lower critical ratio (0.449 vs 0.500) shifts optimal quantity downward by approximately 15 units
    \item \textbf{Shared risk:} SparkFire bears more overage risk, incentivizing better demand forecasting
    \item \textbf{Maintained motivation:} Enhanced discounts/prizes preserve upside potential
    \item \textbf{Cost savings:} Leisure Limited reduces refund costs by \$0.45 per returned unit
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Alternative: Demand Enhancement Contract}

\textbf{Structure:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Leisure Limited invests in regional advertising to increase demand
    \item Shift demand distribution: $D \sim U[150, 450]$ instead of $U[120, 420]$
    \item Increase expedited cost to $c_1 = \$4.00$ (from \$3.60) to fund advertising
    \item Improve signal quality: $P(D = \text{High} \mid S = \text{High}) = 0.9$ (from 0.8)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Rationale:} Higher demand probability and better signal accuracy justify the \$0.40 expedited premium increase. From Task 8 sensitivity analysis, improving $P(D = \text{High} \mid S = \text{High})$ from 0.8 to 0.9 increases VOSRC by approximately \$25, offsetting the cost increase.

\subsubsection{Data Requirements}

To set contract parameters optimally:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Leisure's costs:} Actual cost to refurbish/resell returned inventory (currently unknown)
    \item \textbf{Market data:} Competitor refund policies and their impact on stand participation rates
    \item \textbf{Historical returns:} Distribution of actual return quantities across all stands
    \item \textbf{Advertising ROI:} Cost per unit demand increase and campaign effectiveness
    \item \textbf{Prize impact:} Elasticity of order quantity with respect to prize value
\end{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Shipping cost $s$:} Weak leverage with \$67.80 improvement per \$0.01 decrease
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Explanation: Why Refund Fraction Dominates}

The refund fraction $f$ affects both stages of the two-stage model:

\textbf{Stage 1 impact:} Higher $f$ reduces overage cost $C_o = c_0(1-f) + s$, shifting optimal $Q_0$ upward. This increases pre-season inventory, creating more flexibility for reactive adjustments.

\textbf{Stage 2 impact:} Higher $f$ also reduces overage cost for expedited inventory, making it more attractive to order additional units when Signal High occurs.

\textbf{Compound effect:} Unlike $s$ (which affects both stages symmetrically) or $c_1$ (which only affects Stage 2), the refund fraction creates a multiplicative benefit. At $f = 0.55$, VOSRC reaches \$41.57 because:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Pre-season ordering becomes less risky (lower $C_o$)
    \item Expedited ordering becomes more attractive (lower marginal cost of flexibility)
    \item Both baseline profit AND two-stage profit increase, but two-stage increases more
\end{itemize}

Conversely, at $f = 0.45$, VOSRC collapses to \$8.86 because overage penalties (\$2.45 vs \$2.00) make both pre-season and expedited ordering unattractive, nearly eliminating the signal's value.

\subsubsection{Negotiation Recommendation}

\textbf{Primary target:} Negotiate higher refund fraction $f$ from 0.50 to 0.55 (+10\%)
\begin{itemize}
    \item Expected VOSRC improvement: +\$10.17 (from \$31.40 to \$41.57)
    \item Leverage argument: Share cost savings from reduced expedited orders (better pre-season planning reduces rush orders)
    \item Fallback: Request increase to $f = 0.525$ (+5\%) for \$5 improvement
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Secondary target:} If refund negotiation fails, reduce expedited cost $c_1$ from \$3.60 to \$3.24 (-10\%)
\begin{itemize}
    \item Expected VOSRC improvement: +\$27.88 (from \$31.40 to \$59.28)
    \item Leverage argument: Volume commitment---guarantee minimum expedited order quantity in exchange for lower rate
    \item Risk: Requires binding commitment that may backfire if Signal Low occurs
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Avoid:} Shipping cost $s$ negotiation offers minimal return (\$67.80 per \$0.01) and may antagonize Leisure Limited over a small operational cost they cannot easily reduce.
